Thanks everyone. Today I'm presenting the AI Agents Academic Research Assistant — a project I built to automate the most painful parts of literature research in the AI agents field. Instead of spending days reading papers just to form a single hypothesis, this system takes a research question and returns a full research brief: relevant papers, synthesized themes, novel hypotheses that have already been stress-tested, and a concrete experiment design. Let me walk you through how it works.

Why we built this

The Problem

Information overload

AI agents is one of the fastest-growing subfields. Thousands of new papers per month — impossible to track manually.

Keyword search is shallow

Search engines return what matches your query. They don't know what's foundational, what contradicts what, or what's missing.

Synthesis is slow

Reading 20 papers, identifying themes, spotting gaps — that's days of work before you can even form a hypothesis.

Hypotheses go unchallenged

Most researchers don't have an adversary who reads the literature and argues against their ideas before they invest months in an experiment.

The motivation came from a very real set of frustrations. First, information overload. AI agents is one of the hottest subfields right now — which means thousands of new papers every month. Nobody can keep up. Second, keyword search is fundamentally shallow. You search for "tool-using LLM agents" and get papers containing those words — but miss the 2018 paper that introduced the concept everyone is building on, and the 2024 paper showing the approach doesn't generalize outside benchmarks. Third, even if you find the right papers, synthesizing them is slow. Identifying themes, spotting contradictions, figuring out what's unsolved — that's days of work before you can write a single hypothesis. Fourth — hypotheses go unchallenged. You come up with an idea, it sounds good, and you start building. Nobody tells you a paper from last year already partially answered it, or that your key assumption doesn't hold.

05 / 12
Nodes 1 – 3

Finding the Right Papers

🔍 search_node — parallel retrieval

Queries Semantic Scholar and ArXiv simultaneously using Python threads. Returns up to 20 deduplicated papers. On retry, auto-broadens the query by stripping the most specific term.

⚖️ critic_node — Claude relevance filter

Claude scores every paper 1–10 against the research question with a one-line reason. Only papers scoring ≥ 6 proceed. If fewer than 5 pass, the loop retries with a broader query (max 2×).

❄️ snowball_node — citation graph expansion

Fetches references and citations for the top 4 relevant papers from the Semantic Scholar graph API — surfacing foundational work and recent follow-ups that keyword search misses entirely.

Search fires two API calls simultaneously using Python threads — deduplicates by normalized title. On retry, strips the longest term automatically. "Multi-agent reinforcement learning cooperation" becomes "multi-agent learning cooperation" without user input. Critic sends all twenty papers to Claude alongside the research question, gets a score and one-line reason for each. Below six gets dropped. First quality gate. Snowball is the most novel piece here. For the top four relevant papers, we hit the Semantic Scholar graph API and fetch both their references — what they cite — and their citations — who cites them. These are papers keyword search never surfaces, but they're what the field is actually built on.

Node 4

Grounded Synthesis

The core problem with LLM synthesis: it invents plausible-sounding themes that aren't actually in the papers. We solve this with mandatory citation indices.

What Claude must return

themes: [{
  "claim": "Tool-use agents...",
  "paper_indices": [0, 3, 7]
}]
gaps: [...]
contradictions: [...]
open_questions: [...]

Validation step

  • After Claude responds, each claim's paper_indices are checked against the actual paper list
  • Any claim where all indices are out of range is silently dropped
  • Valid indices are mapped to titles and shown as citation badges in the UI
If you just ask Claude to synthesize themes it will — and it sounds convincing. But some of those themes will be plausible-sounding ideas Claude knows from training data, not from the papers you gave it. That's a fundamental problem for research. Our fix: mandatory citation indices. Every claim must include paper_indices pointing to real papers in our list. After Claude responds, we validate every index. If all the indices are out of range — meaning Claude invented references — the claim is silently dropped. Only claims traceable to real documents survive. In the UI you can hover citation badges and see exactly which papers support each theme. This turns a black-box output into something verifiable.

Nodes 5 – 6 · The key reasoning loop

Adversarial Hypothesis Refinement

↺ up to 2 iterations Triggers when any hypothesis scores < 6 viability
💡 hypothesis_node

Pass 1: generates 3 novel hypotheses from the synthesis.
Pass 2+: receives prior hypotheses and their critiques side-by-side. Prompt instructs Claude to produce "meaningfully different and stronger" replacements that directly address each counterargument.

⚔️ advocate_node — devil's advocate

For each hypothesis: lists counterarguments citing specific papers, identifies logical flaws and untestable assumptions, produces a refined claim, and assigns a viability score 1–10. The graph routes back if any score < 6.

This is the part I'm most proud of — the adversarial refinement loop. This is what makes the graph genuinely non-linear. Hypothesis node generates three research hypotheses from the synthesis on the first pass. Then Advocate plays devil's advocate. For each hypothesis it lists specific counterarguments — often citing papers by index — flags logical flaws and untestable assumptions, produces a refined version, and assigns a viability score. Here's the key: if any score comes back below six, the graph routes back to Hypothesis node. But on the second pass it doesn't start fresh — it receives the previous hypotheses and their critiques side by side, and the prompt explicitly instructs Claude to generate "meaningfully different and stronger" replacements that address the specific counterarguments. This is a real feedback loop between two agents. The generator learns from the critic. Up to two iterations.

Node 7

Experiment Design

The hypothesis that best survived adversarial review (highest viability score) is selected — not always hypothesis #1.

What gets designed
  • Step-by-step methodology
  • Baseline to compare against
  • Concrete metrics for success
  • Specific datasets to use
  • Failure modes + how to detect them

Selection logic

top = max(hypotheses,
  key=lambda h:
    h["viability_score"])

Viability is set by the advocate after the final review round — meaning the experiment is always built on the hypothesis that best withstood critique.

The final node designs a concrete, reproducible experiment. The selection step is important — it doesn't take hypothesis number one. It takes max(hypotheses, key=viability_score) — the one that best survived adversarial review. If hypothesis three turned out most defensible, the experiment is designed around that. Output covers: step-by-step methodology, baseline to compare against, concrete measurable metrics, specific dataset recommendations, and failure modes — what could go wrong and how you'd detect it early. All structured JSON, validated on parse.

Under the hood

Tech Stack

LangGraph

StateGraph with typed state, conditional edges, and native loop support. Both retry loops are first-class graph edges — not hacks in application logic.

Anthropic Claude

claude-sonnet-4-20250514. Every agent has a distinct system prompt. All outputs are structured JSON with a retry-on-parse-failure fallback.

Semantic Scholar + ArXiv

S2 Graph API for paper search and citation graph traversal (free, no auth). ArXiv Python package for parallel retrieval. Deduplication by normalized title.

Streamlit

Custom dark theme via config.toml + injected CSS. Live node status using a background thread + queue. No page refresh needed.

LangGraph for the graph. The two loops are conditional edges defined in the graph — not if-statements in application code. That means the graph is inspectable and reproducible. Claude Sonnet for all reasoning. Every agent has a distinct system prompt. Structured JSON output with a retry-on-parse-failure mechanism built in. Semantic Scholar and ArXiv are both free, no authentication required. Streamlit for the UI — live pipeline tracker using a background thread feeding a queue, so the graph and UI run independently without blocking each other.

Design decisions

Three Rigor Improvements

1 · Citation Snowballing

After the critic selects relevant papers, we fetch their references and citations from the S2 graph API in parallel. This surfaces foundational work and recent follow-ups that keyword search is blind to — improving everything downstream without extra Claude calls.

2 · Grounded Synthesis

Synthesis claims must cite paper indices. Uncited claims are dropped post-response. This closes the hallucination gap: Claude can only assert things traceable to documents we actually retrieved.

3 · Adversarial Refinement Loop

The advocate isn't decorative — weak hypotheses trigger a real second pass where Claude sees its own critiques and must produce stronger alternatives. The experiment is built on a hypothesis that survived attack, not just one that was generated.

Three decisions that moved this from a prototype to something you can trust. Citation snowballing: adding citation graph expansion was a single API addition, but it fundamentally changes corpus quality — you stop being limited to papers containing your exact query terms. Grounded synthesis: the original had no validation. The hallucination problem wasn't obvious because the output sounded plausible. Adding index validation costs nothing at runtime but makes every claim traceable. The adversarial loop: the original had a one-shot advocate whose critiques nobody acted on. Making critiques feed back into a second hypothesis generation pass was the biggest architectural change — and made the biggest difference to output quality. The hypotheses after iteration two are consistently more specific and harder to dismiss.

What's next

Future Work

More rigour

  • Human-in-the-loop checkpoint after critic — let the researcher prune or add papers before synthesis
  • Synthesis quality loop — score grounding density, retry if too vague
  • Multi-round debate — hypothesis and advocate trade turns, not just one challenge
  • Statistical power analysis in experiment design

More capability

  • Persistent sessions — save and resume runs, compare across questions
  • Additional sources — ACL Anthology, PubMed, IEEE Xplore
  • LaTeX export — related work section ready to drop into a paper
  • Embedding-based deduplication — catch near-duplicate titles missed by string matching
A few directions this could go next. On rigor: a human-in-the-loop checkpoint after the critic so a researcher can prune false positives before synthesis. A synthesis quality loop that scores grounding density and retries if too vague. Multi-round debate — hypothesis and advocate trading turns until convergence. On capability: persistent sessions to compare runs across questions. ACL Anthology for NLP-specific work. LaTeX export so the related work section drops straight into a paper draft.
